# The Interview Format & Capacity Estimation

A system design interview is **45 minutes of structured conversation**, not a drawing contest. The loop your interviewer follows:

1. **Requirements (5–8 min)** — functional ("users can book a ride") and non-functional (scale, latency, consistency, availability). *Skipping this is the #1 senior-candidate failure.*
2. **Capacity estimation (3–5 min)** — back-of-envelope math to justify later choices.
3. **API + data model (5 min)** — a few key endpoints and entities.
4. **High-level design (10 min)** — boxes and arrows: clients, gateway, services, stores, queues.
5. **Deep dives (15 min)** — the interviewer picks 2–3 components; this is where levels are decided.
6. **Wrap-up** — bottlenecks, failure modes, what you'd do next.

## Numbers to memorize
- ~100K seconds/day (actually 86,400 — round for speed).
- 1M requests/day ≈ **12 RPS** average; design for 5–10× peak.
- Reads typically outnumber writes 10:1 to 100:1.
- A single PostgreSQL node comfortably handles thousands of simple QPS — don't shard at 100 RPS.
- Latency ladder: memory ~100ns, SSD ~100µs, same-region network ~1ms, cross-region ~50–150ms.

## Worked example
10M DAU × 10 requests/day = 100M req/day ≈ **1,000 RPS average, ~5–10K peak**. If each profile is 1 KB and you store 100M users → 100 GB — fits one DB with replicas; sharding is *not yet justified*. Saying "we don't need to shard yet, and here's the math" scores higher than premature complexity.

## Lab
Estimate RPS, storage/year, and bandwidth for: (a) a URL shortener at 100M new links/month, (b) FastTrack Relay if it served every UAE airport. Write both on one page — this becomes your warm-up ritual before real interviews.
