# Cost, Resilience & Well-Architected Reviews

Architects get hired to balance five pillars under a budget. The Azure Well-Architected Framework (WAF) gives you the structure; this lesson makes it conversational.

## The five pillars, one sentence each
- **Reliability:** design for failure — zones, health probes, retries with backoff + circuit breakers, tested (not assumed) recovery.
- **Security:** identity-first, least privilege, encrypt everywhere, private networking.
- **Cost optimization:** right-size, reserve, autoscale to zero where possible, delete idle.
- **Operational excellence:** IaC, CI/CD, observability, runbooks, post-incident reviews.
- **Performance efficiency:** scale horizontally, cache deliberately, measure before optimizing (your 139s→28s and 60s→365ms stories live here).

## Resilience patterns checklist
Retry (with jitter!) → timeout → circuit breaker → bulkhead → graceful degradation. In .NET: `Microsoft.Extensions.Resilience` / Polly pipelines. The senior phrasing: *"Retries without budgets and jitter are a self-inflicted DDoS."* Define a health model per user-facing flow: what does 'healthy booking flow' mean in metrics (p99 latency, error rate, queue depth), and alert on that — not on CPU.

## Cost fluency (the most under-prepared topic)
- Compute is usually 60–70% of the bill: right-size first, then Reserved Instances/Savings Plans (30–60% off) for steady load, spot for batch.
- Non-prod environments off at night = ~65% saved on dev/test compute. One Terraform schedule does it.
- Egress and cross-zone traffic are the silent line items; log analytics ingestion is the other.
- Always present designs with a rough monthly figure: "this resilience tier costs ~$X more; the outage it prevents costs $Y/hour."
