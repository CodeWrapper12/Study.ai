# LAB: Strangler-Fig — Split a Monolith

The most asked architect-interview scenario in the Gulf market (everyone has legacy): *"We have a monolith. How do you modernize without a rewrite?"* You've lived pieces of this (back-office migration); this lab makes your answer systematic.

## The strangler-fig pattern
Put a routing facade (your YARP gateway) in front of the monolith. Carve out one capability at a time into a new service; flip its routes at the gateway; the monolith shrinks until it's gone. No big-bang, reversible at every step.

## The exercise (paper + partial code, ~2 evenings)
Take a hypothetical "TravelOffice" monolith (bookings, invoicing, reporting, notifications — modeled on systems you've actually touched):

1. **Pick the first cut** and justify it. Good first cuts: high change-rate + low coupling (notifications). Bad: the booking core (highest coupling, do it last). Write the reasoning down — interviewers care about the *ordering logic* more than the boxes.
2. **Data separation strategy:** new service gets its own schema/DB; during transition use views or sync events from the monolith DB. Name the anti-pattern you're avoiding: shared-database integration.
3. **Routing plan:** YARP config showing `/api/notifications/*` → new service, everything else → monolith. Include a kill switch (route flag) to revert instantly.
4. **The seam problem:** the monolith still *calls* notification logic internally. Options: anti-corruption layer + HTTP/queue call back to the new service, or events from the monolith (outbox on the legacy DB). You've built an ACL before — this is its second use.
5. **Implement the slice:** extract Notifications from a stub monolith into a service behind your RelayFlow gateway, with a feature-flag route flip.

## Deliverable
A `docs/strangler-plan.md` in the portfolio repo: ordering rationale, data strategy, routing config, rollback plan. This single document is reusable almost verbatim in interviews and even in your first month at the next job.
