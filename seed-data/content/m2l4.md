# Queues, Streams & Backpressure

Async messaging appears in ~80% of design interviews ("how do you handle the spike?"). This lesson gives you the interview vocabulary; M3 makes you dangerous in .NET specifically.

## Why a queue
- **Decoupling:** producer doesn't know or wait for consumers.
- **Load leveling:** absorb a 10× spike, drain at sustainable rate.
- **Resilience:** consumer down ≠ data lost.
The trade: eventual processing, duplicate delivery, and ordering complexity.

## Queue vs log
- **Queue (RabbitMQ, SQS):** message consumed → gone. Competing consumers for work distribution.
- **Log (Kafka, Event Hubs):** append-only, retained, offset-based. Multiple independent consumer groups replay the same stream — enables analytics + search-indexing + notifications from one event stream.
Rule of thumb to say out loud: *commands → queue, facts/events → log.*

## Delivery semantics
At-most-once (loss ok), at-least-once (duplicates — the practical default), "exactly-once" (a myth across system boundaries; you build *effectively-once* with at-least-once + idempotent consumers).

## Backpressure
What happens when producers outrun consumers? Bounded queues + reject/shed at the edge, autoscale consumers on queue depth, or drop low-value work (sampling). Naming backpressure unprompted is a staff-level signal.

## Dead letters
Bounded retries with exponential backoff → DLQ → alert + replay tooling. Never infinite-retry a poison message.

## Lab
Design the notification fan-out for FastTrack Relay (booking confirmed → email, SMS, partner webhook, audit): choose queue or log per consumer, define retry/DLQ policy, and state what happens during a 30-minute SMS-provider outage.
