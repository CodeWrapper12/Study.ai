# Scaling Writes: Sharding, Partitioning & Consistency

When one primary can't absorb writes, you partition. This topic separates senior from staff answers.

## Sharding fundamentals
- **Shard key selection is THE decision.** Good: high-cardinality, evenly distributed, matches dominant query (e.g., `user_id` for a user-centric app). Bad: date (today's shard is hot), sequential IDs, celebrity-skewed keys.
- **Hash vs range:** hash distributes evenly but kills range scans; range supports scans but invites hot spots.
- **Consistent hashing** keeps resharding cheap — only ~1/n keys move when adding a node. Know the ring diagram well enough to draw it.
- **Cross-shard queries and transactions** become application problems: scatter-gather reads, sagas instead of distributed transactions (you'll build these in M3).

## Consistency vocabulary
- **CAP:** under partition, choose consistency (reject some requests) or availability (serve possibly-stale data). Real systems choose per-operation, not globally.
- **Strong vs eventual:** booking payments → strong; like-counts and dashboards → eventual.
- **Quorums (N/R/W):** R + W > N gives read-your-writes overlap. One sentence on this covers most interview needs.
- **Idempotency keys** on write APIs — retries must not double-charge. You can cite your partner API's design here.

## The senior move
Don't shard in the interview until the math forces you to — and when you do, name the shard key, the rebalancing story, and the cross-shard query you've just made expensive.

## Lab
For a payments ledger at 50K writes/sec: choose a shard key, justify it, identify the hot-spot risk, and describe how an account-to-account transfer works across shards. One page.
